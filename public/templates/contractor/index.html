How Contractors Should Structure Invoices

Contractor projects often involve complex resource calculations. Keep your invoicing precise by using these formatting rules:

  • Separate Labor & Materials: Break down labor hours (e.g. "Drywall installation - 12 hours") separately from material supplies (e.g. "Paint, rollers, primers").
  • Verify Local Sales Tax: Depending on your municipality, materials and services may have different tax structures. Calculate these properly in the Totals area.
  • Add Project/Site Details: Always specify the exact physical site address where the contract was executed in the Notes section.

Common Mistakes in Contractor Billing

Ensure quick approval from your clients by avoiding these three critical invoicing errors:

1. Forgetting to Track Partial Payments

If you received an upfront deposit, always specify the "Amount Paid" in the totals sidebar to ensure the final "Balance Due" matches the outstanding agreement.

2. Unitemized Lump Sum Billing

Writing a single line for "General Labor - $5,000" causes payment disputes. Detail the breakdown of materials and labor parameters.

3. Poor Date Tracking

Always log the exact date range the project took place to make it easy for the client's accounting department to cross-reference with internal work orders.

Contractor Payment Cycles

Standard payment intervals for contractors focus heavily on material cost coverage:

  • Due Upon Receipt: Best for rapid residential repairs or small materials-only deliveries.
  • Net 15: The commercial subcontractor standard, giving the general contractor two weeks to process funding.
  • Progress Invoicing: Invoicing at predefined stages of the project (e.g., 30% framing complete, 30% drywall complete, 40% final inspection).
