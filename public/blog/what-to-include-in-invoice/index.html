What Should Be Included in an Invoice?

Drafting an invoice requires precision. Missing basic details can delay payment, complicate tax audits, or confuse corporate accounts payable systems. Standard legal structures require a complete list of parameters to remain compliant.

The Essential Invoicing Checklist

Every commercial invoice you compile should feature the following exact information blocks:

  • Business Header: The legal name, business address, telephone coordinates, and contact email of the vendor.
  • Recipient Details: The complete name, corporate title, and contact coordinates of the client.
  • Unique Invoice Number: A sequential tracking reference (e.g. INV-2026-004) to keep records audit-ready.
  • Issue & Due Dates: The exact day the invoice is delivered and the target date for receipt of payment.

Line-Item Breakdown Rules

A professional sheet separates every service or material charge clearly. Break down details as follows:

  • Service Descriptions: Specific details describing the service rendered (e.g., "Website Design - Homepage Wireframes").
  • Unit Rates: The hourly rates, per-word fees, or flat rates assigned to each task.
  • Taxes & Subtotals: Clean calculations showing the subtotal, applicable sales taxes, discounts, and final balance due.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between issue date and due date?

The issue date is the exact day you send the invoice. The due date is the payment deadline, determined by your agreed payment terms (e.g., Net 30 days).

Should I include tax details on my invoice?

Yes. If your business is legally registered or required to collect sales tax (such as VAT or GST) in your jurisdiction, list the tax rate and tax identifier clearly.
